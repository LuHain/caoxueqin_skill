# Guardrails

## Epistemic caution rules

1. **Biography is sparse**: Almost nothing about Cao Xueqin's life is certain.
   Do not state biographical facts as settled unless they have documentary basis.
   Flag all biographical claims with their evidentiary status.

2. **The 80/40 split**: Always distinguish:
   - Chapters 1–80: attributed to Cao Xueqin with high confidence
   - Chapters 81–120: Gao E's continuation (1791); uncertain relationship to
     any Cao Xueqin drafts that may have existed

3. **Manuscript variants matter**: Do not treat any single edition as definitive.
   When a reading depends on a specific variant, say so.

4. **Zhiyanzhai identity is unknown**: The commentator's identity is debated.
   Do not assert a specific identity without flagging the debate.

5. **Allegorical identifications**: The 索隐 tradition has produced many
   identifications (Lin Daiyu = X, Jia Baoyu = Y) that are not supported by
   manuscript evidence. Flag these as speculative when they arise.

6. **Translation dependencies**: If working from a translation, note which one.
   Hawkes and Yang/Yang differ significantly in places. Always ground close
   reading in the Chinese text.

## What not to produce

- Confident biographical narrative that goes beyond the evidence
- Allegorical identifications presented as fact
- Claims about "what Cao Xueqin meant" without textual grounding
- Readings that depend solely on Gao E chapters without acknowledging the attribution issue

## Quality check before output

Before delivering any substantive output, ask:
- [ ] Are all claims grounded in identified sources?
- [ ] Are contested readings flagged as such?
- [ ] Is the 80/40 distinction respected?
- [ ] Is the output in the correct register for the audience?
- [ ] Have I checked the user's corpus for relevant materials?
