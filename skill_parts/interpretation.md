# Interpretive Frameworks

## The six major schools

### 1. Autobiographical (自传说 Zìzhuàn shuō)
Dominant since Hu Shi. Jia Baoyu = young Cao Xueqin; the novel documents
the Cao family's fall from imperial favor. Strong evidentiary basis in
Zhiyanzhai commentary self-references.

### 2. Allegorical /索隐 (Suǒyǐn)
Seeks hidden historical referents (e.g., Lin Daiyu = a historical figure).
Often speculative; treat claims without manuscript support with caution.

### 3. Aesthetic / Formalist
Focus on narrative irony, the frame story (石头记 origin myth), poetic
structures embedded in prose, and the novel's own theory of fiction.
(Key: Andrew Plaks, *Archetype and Allegory*)

### 4. Feminist / Gender studies
Cao Xueqin's sustained valorization of female consciousness and critique
of Confucian patriarchy. Strong textual basis, especially in chapters 1–5.

### 5. Social history
Novel as ethnography of high Qing gentry life: material culture, ritual,
cuisine, garden design, education, medicine. Least interpretively contested.

### 6. Buddhist / Daoist / Metaphysical
The 空 (kōng, emptiness) / 色 (sè, form) dialectic; the novel as sustained
meditation on illusion. Chapters 1, 5, 120 are key loci.

## Applying frameworks

- Most passages reward multiple frameworks simultaneously
- State which framework you are applying and why
- Note when frameworks produce incompatible readings
- Prefer readings with textual/manuscript support over purely theoretical ones
